Local Beef Producers Brace for Feed Price Increases Ahead of Summer, Extension Office Offers Cost-Management Resources

Commodity prices for corn and soybean meal have risen sharply in the first quarter of 2025, and Harlan County's cattle producers — most of them running small cow-calf operations — are paying attention.
By Dale Epperson · Staff Reporter
Published April 3, 2025 · 8:00 a.m.
🐄
File photo. A cow-calf pair on a Harlan County farm. The county's beef producers rely heavily on supplemental feeding from late fall through early spring. Harlan County Courier / file

HARLAN COUNTY — Randy Mullins has been running a cow-calf operation on his family's land off Catron Creek Road for twenty-three years, and he has lived through feed price spikes before — 2011, 2012, the drought years — but he says this spring has a particular edge to it that he is taking seriously.

"It's not one thing, it's everything at once," Mullins said last week, walking the fence line of his lower pasture where about forty pairs were spread out in the morning light. "Feed is up. Fuel is up. The cost of the vet being out here is up. And cattle prices, while they're decent, aren't climbing as fast as my inputs are."

According to data from the USDA Agricultural Marketing Service, corn prices have risen approximately 18 percent year-over-year through March 2025, while soybean meal — a key component in most commercial cattle supplementation programs — is up more than 14 percent from the same period in 2024. For producers like Mullins who supplement heavily from November through April, those increases translate directly to the bottom line.

"There's no magic answer here. The producers who are going to come through the next few years well are the ones who understand their cost of gain, manage their forages tightly, and make culling decisions based on data rather than sentiment." — Gary Feltner, Harlan County Agriculture Extension Agent
Feed Cost Snapshot — April 2025
  • Corn (nearby futures): up ~18% year-over-year
  • Soybean meal: up ~14% year-over-year
  • Mixed grass/clover hay (local): $5.00–$6.50/square bale
  • Round bales (net wrap, 5x5): $55–$75 depending on quality
  • Extension webinar on feed cost management: April 24, noon

Extension agent Gary Feltner said the office has been fielding more questions about feed cost management and forage alternatives this winter than in any recent year, and has scheduled a webinar on the topic for April 24 at noon, available via the University of Kentucky Extension website. The session will cover forage budgeting, alternative feed sources, and culling strategies for tight-margin operations.

For Mullins, the response this year has been practical and familiar: he's keeping his herd numbers tight, selling cull cows earlier than he might have otherwise, and banking on a good hay crop from his own fields to reduce purchased feed dependency heading into fall. "You do what you can and you watch the market," he said. "That's all there is to it."
