Ridgeline Petting Farm Opens for the Season Saturday with New Pygmy Goat Exhibit and Expanded Weekend Hours

After a longer-than-usual winter hiatus, the Ridgeline Petting Farm on Old Clear Creek Road is welcoming visitors again — and this year they have twelve new pygmy goats to show for the wait.
By Meredith A. Holloway · Staff Reporter
Published April 3, 2025 · 9:00 a.m.
🐐
File photo, Ridgeline Petting Farm. The farm's new pygmy goat enclosure, completed in February, houses twelve animals acquired from two breeders in Tennessee. The exhibit is open to visitors beginning this Saturday. Photo courtesy of Ridgeline Petting Farm

HARLAN COUNTY — Sandra and Pete Whitaker have been opening their petting farm on Old Clear Creek Road every spring since 2009, and in all that time, Sandra says, she has never seen her husband more excited about an addition to the property than he is about the twelve pygmy goats currently occupying the new enclosure on the south end of the barn.

"He drove to two different farms in Tennessee in January to pick them out personally," Sandra said on Wednesday, leaning against the fence of the new pen as one of the smaller does attempted to sample her jacket sleeve. "That's how you know he's serious about something."

The pygmy goats — six does, four wethers, and two young males — range in age from eight months to three years and represent a mix of classic agouti patterning and the lighter caramel colorations that Pete says tend to be particularly popular with younger visitors. They arrived at Ridgeline in two batches over February and March and have spent the intervening weeks settling into their new surroundings, which Pete designed with low climbing platforms, a covered feeding area, and a separate smaller pen that allows guests to interact with the animals directly under staff supervision.

"A pygmy goat is about as interactive as a farm animal gets. They want your attention. They're curious. They'll follow you around. For a kid who's never been on a farm, that first time a goat looks you in the eye and decides you're interesting — that's a real moment." — Pete Whitaker, Ridgeline Petting Farm
Ridgeline Petting Farm — 2025 Season

  • Hours: Saturdays and Sundays, 10:00 a.m. – 4:00 p.m.

A Farm Built for Connection

Ridgeline Petting Farm has operated as a seasonal attraction since 2009, when Sandra and Pete converted a portion of their working cattle and hay property into a guest-facing experience aimed at families and school groups. Over the years the roster of animals has grown and shifted — they had alpacas for three seasons, a pair of miniature donkeys that proved to be more opinionated than advertised, and a potbellied pig named Franklin who held court in his own enclosure for nine years before his death in 2022.

The current lineup, alongside the new pygmy goats, includes rabbits, heritage-breed chickens, domestic ducks, two Nigerian Dwarf goats who have been with the farm since 2018, and a pair of Shetland ponies that Sandra describes as "retired but not uninterested in carrots."

This year the farm is also expanding its weekday school visit program, which has historically been available by appointment but was limited by scheduling constraints. Starting this spring, Tuesday through Friday appointments will be available through a new online booking system on the farm's website.

"We get a lot of calls from teachers who want to bring kids out during the week," Sandra said. "This year we put the infrastructure in place to actually do it consistently. That was Pete's winter project, when he wasn't in Tennessee picking out goats."

Ridgeline opens to the public this Saturday, April 5, and will operate Saturdays and Sundays from 10:00 a.m. to 4:00 p.m. through the end of October. Admission is $6 per person, with children under two admitted free.
