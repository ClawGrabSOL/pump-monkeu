Harlan City Council Approves $2.1 Million Community Center Renovation Budget in 6-1 Vote

After two months of public comment and three rounds of budget revision, the council approved the full renovation package Thursday night — the largest single capital expenditure the city has approved since the 2018 water system upgrade.
By Dale Epperson · Staff Reporter
Published April 4, 2025 · 11:30 p.m.
🏛
The Harlan Community Center on Central Street will undergo a full renovation beginning this summer after the city council approved a $2.1 million budget Thursday night. Harlan County Courier / file

HARLAN — The Harlan City Council voted 6-1 Thursday night to approve a $2.1 million renovation budget for the Harlan Community Center on Central Street, ending a process that began in February when the mayor's office first presented the scope of work to council members and a visibly divided public response followed.

The approved package includes full HVAC replacement, roof repair, accessible entrance and bathroom upgrades to bring the 1974-era building into ADA compliance, resurfacing of the gymnasium floor, and renovations to the commercial kitchen that the building's nonprofit users have said is effectively non-functional in its current condition.

The single dissenting vote came from Councilwoman Pat Brock, who said she supported the renovation in principle but remained concerned about the project's contingency budget, which she characterized as "thin for a building that age." Brock proposed an amendment to increase the contingency reserve from five percent to ten percent, but the amendment failed 4-3 before the final budget vote passed.

"We've put this off long enough. Every year we wait, that building gets more expensive to fix and less useful to the people who depend on it." — Mayor David Saylor

Construction is expected to begin in late June following a competitive bidding process. The city has budgeted for the community center to remain closed for approximately five months during construction, with a target reopening in December 2025.

Funding for the project comes from a combination of a $1.4 million federal Community Development Block Grant awarded last fall, $500,000 in city general fund reserves, and $200,000 in state rural development funds administered through the Kentucky Department of Local Government.
