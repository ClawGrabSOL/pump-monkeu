Editorial · The Courier Editorial Board

Our Farmers Deserve Better Than a State Budget That Forgets Them

Another session in Frankfort has come and gone, and once again Harlan County's agricultural community is looking at a budget that offers platitudes and very little else.
The Harlan County Courier Editorial Board
April 6, 2025

There is something deeply familiar about the moment after a legislative session closes and our county's farmers sit down to read what has been allocated — and what has not. That familiar feeling is disappointment, and this year it arrived right on schedule.

The 2025-26 state budget, passed last week by the Kentucky General Assembly, includes a modest increase in general agricultural support through the Department of Agriculture but fails in two critical ways that matter specifically to rural, small-operation counties like ours. The first is the near-elimination of the Rural Agricultural Revitalization Fund, a targeted grant program that has helped twelve Harlan County farms upgrade equipment, install irrigation systems, or transition to new crops over the last four years. The second is the continued absence of any meaningful investment in rural veterinary access — a crisis that anyone trying to get a large-animal vet out to a farm on the eastern end of this county understands with frustrating intimacy.

We do not make this argument lightly. The Courier has consistently supported the current administration on issues of workforce development and infrastructure. But support is not unconditional, and this budget tests it.

Agriculture is not a heritage program. It is not a tourism bullet point. It is a living economy that feeds families — including families in this county who have no other reliable income. — The Harlan County Courier Editorial Board

The farms of Harlan County are not large industrial operations. They are family properties, many of them multigenerational, producing cattle, hay, produce and specialty crops on land that has been worked continuously for over a century. The people who run them do not need ribbon-cutting ceremonies. They need access to credit, veterinary care, soil science resources, and, occasionally, a grant program that does not require a law firm to apply for.

We call on our state representatives — who both serve on agriculture-related committees — to bring this back to the floor in the next session. The farmers of this county are watching. So are we.

Letter to the Editor
Thank You to the Volunteers Who Showed Up for the April 2nd Harlan Creek Cleanup
Submitted by Norma Jean Hoskins, Evarts
April 4, 2025

I wanted to take a moment to publicly thank the 34 volunteers who gave up their Wednesday morning to help clean the stretch of Harlan Creek between the old Slusher property and the Route 38 bridge. We collected eleven bags of trash, two tires, and what appeared to be a lawn mower that had seen better days.

These are the kinds of efforts that don't make big headlines, but they matter enormously to those of us who live along the creek and have watched it slowly improve over the past three years of organized cleanups. A special thank-you to the students from Evarts Middle School who came out with their science teacher, Mr. Dobbs — you were the hardest workers out there, and don't let anyone tell you different.

The next cleanup is tentatively scheduled for June 7. Anyone who would like to be added to the notification list can email me directly at the address below.

— Norma Jean Hoskins, Evarts, KY  ·  njeanhoskins@gmail.com

Column · Down the Road
Spring Comes Slow Up Here, and That's the Way We Like It
By Carl "Buck" Hubbard, Contributing Columnist
April 5, 2025

You can always tell when spring has made up its mind in Harlan County. It's not the calendar — the calendar has been lying about spring for as long as I can remember, telling you it arrived on March 20 while the frost is still going at the windshield. The real sign is the way the ridgeline looks in the morning light when the first real warmth comes through. It changes color. The whole thing softens.

I've been watching that ridgeline from my back porch for forty-one years now, and it still stops me short every time.

People who grew up somewhere flat don't understand what it means to have a mountain between you and the rest of the world. It's not a barrier. It's a reference point. You always know where you are.
