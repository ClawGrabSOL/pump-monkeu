Meet PumpFun: The Millbrook Road Farm's Tiny New Resident Who Stole the Family's Heart — and Its Name From a 14-Year-Old

When the Calloway family brought home a squirrel monkey this past February, they had no idea what to call him. Then their son Tyler looked at that unmistakable green-gold fur and said, without hesitation, "That's PumpFun."
By Meredith A. Holloway · Staff Reporter
Published April 6, 2025 · 7:30 a.m.
PumpFun perches on a branch inside his enclosure at the Calloway family farm on Millbrook Road. The squirrel monkey's distinctive olive-gold fur and wide, dark eyes quickly won over the whole family — none more so than 14-year-old Tyler Calloway, who came up with his name. Photo: Harlan County Courier / Dale Epperson

HARLAN COUNTY — It started the way most things do on the Calloway farm: quietly, practically, and without a whole lot of fuss. Dean and Patrice Calloway had spent the better part of last year looking into adding an exotic animal to their twelve-acre property on Millbrook Road — not for show, they'll tell you, but because their youngest son had been begging for one since the fourth grade.

"Tyler has always had a thing for animals," said Patrice, 44, pouring coffee at the kitchen table while her husband Dean nodded from across the room. "Not just regular farm animals either. We've had goats, pigs, a llama for about six months — God rest her soul. He always wanted something a little different."

What they ended up with is a male squirrel monkey, roughly three years old, acquired through a licensed exotic animal breeder out of central Florida in late February. He arrived on a Tuesday afternoon in a ventilated travel crate, bright-eyed and alert, and according to Dean, 47, made himself at home on a branch inside his climate-controlled enclosure within the first twenty minutes.

Naming him, however, was the subject of a family debate that lasted nearly a week.

"He looked at the monkey for maybe thirty seconds and just went, 'That's PumpFun.' Didn't explain it, didn't back it up. Just walked away like it was done." — Patrice Calloway, on son Tyler's naming of the monkey

"We were going back and forth — Dean wanted Biscuit, I had written down a few names, our daughter Emma suggested Captain — and then Tyler walks in from school, drops his bag, looks at the enclosure, and just says it," Patrice recalled, laughing. "PumpFun. We all kind of looked at each other."

Tyler, now 14 and a freshman at Harlan County High, is not a boy known for long explanations. But when pressed by this reporter on how he arrived at the name, he shrugged and gestured at the monkey's coat.

"Look at him," Tyler said. "He's green. He looks like he's always about to do something. The name just fit."

About Squirrel Monkeys
  • Native to the tropical forests of Central and South America
  • Among the most intelligent of the New World primates
  • Adults typically weigh between 500g and 1.1kg
  • Known for their olive-yellow to golden-green dorsal fur
  • Highly social — thrive in groups of 10 to 500 in the wild
  • Lifespan of 15–20 years in captivity with proper care
  • Require exotic animal permits in most U.S. states

Tyler isn't wrong about the green. PumpFun's coat is one of the more striking features you'll notice the moment you see him — a warm, saturated olive-gold across his back and limbs that shifts toward a softer cream on his chest and belly. His face follows the classic squirrel monkey pattern: a snow-white mask framing wide, ink-dark eyes, anchored by a jet-black nose and mouth patch that gives him a look of permanent, expressive alertness.

"The green is what does it," Dean said. "Soon as the light hits him a certain way, he glows. That's the only word for it. And PumpFun — I don't know — it grew on me fast. By day two I was already saying it like I'd always known him."

A Family Effort Two Years in the Making

The road to PumpFun was not a short one. Dean and Patrice spent roughly two years researching the legality, cost, and practical demands of keeping a small primate before committing to the purchase. Squirrel monkeys are legal to own in their county under a state-issued exotic animal permit, which the Calloways obtained last October after a property inspection and a veterinary review.

"You don't just go buy a monkey," Dean said plainly. "Or you shouldn't, anyway. We talked to a vet, we joined a couple of online groups, we read everything we could find. It's a serious commitment. These animals live fifteen, twenty years. You're taking that on."

PumpFun's enclosure — a custom-built indoor-outdoor structure attached to the south end of the Calloway's main barn — took Dean and his brother-in-law three weekends to complete. It features climbing branches at multiple heights, a heated indoor compartment for cold nights, and what Patrice describes as "way too many enrichment toys" sourced from a specialty supplier in Ohio.

Their veterinarian, Dr. Susan Forde of Millbrook Animal Care, conducted PumpFun's initial health screening and now oversees his ongoing care. "He came to us in excellent condition," Dr. Forde said by phone. "Healthy weight, clear eyes, good temperament. The Calloways have done their homework, and it shows. He's settling in well."

'He Already Knows His Name'

In the six weeks since PumpFun arrived on Millbrook Road, he has become, without much competition, the most talked-about resident on the road. Neighbors have stopped by to see him. Tyler's friends have made the Calloway farm a regular after-school destination. The family's oldest daughter Emma, 17, who claimed early on that she was "not really a monkey person," was observed this past Thursday hand-feeding PumpFun slices of mango while narrating the interaction to her phone camera.

"She's posting him every other day," Patrice said, with a smile that suggested she found this completely unsurprising. "He has more followers than the rest of us combined at this point."

Tyler, for his part, remains the animal's most devoted caretaker. He handles feeding twice daily, cleans the enclosure on weekends, and has begun training PumpFun to respond to basic recall commands — something Dr. Forde says is well within a squirrel monkey's cognitive range.

"He already knows his name," Tyler said, standing at the enclosure mesh on a recent afternoon as PumpFun moved fluidly between branches, pausing occasionally to stare back at him with those enormous, unreadable eyes. "Watch." He stepped back, said it once — PumpFun — and the monkey stopped, turned, and fixed him with the kind of direct, interested gaze that makes you feel, briefly, as if you are the one being evaluated.

Dean watched from a few feet back, arms folded. "Told you," he said quietly, to no one in particular. "That name just fits."
