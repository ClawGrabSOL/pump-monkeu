County Extension Office Reports Stronger-Than-Expected Planting Season Outlook for Eastern District

Soil moisture levels and a late warm stretch have put local producers in a better position heading into spring planting than at any point in the last three years.
By Dale Epperson · Staff Reporter
Published April 5, 2025 · 6:00 a.m.
🌽
File photo. Corn fields in the lower end of Harlan County during last year's growing season. Extension agents say conditions this spring are tracking better than 2022, 2023 or 2024 at the same point in the calendar. Harlan County Courier / file

HARLAN COUNTY — For the first time in several years, the Harlan County Cooperative Extension Service is entering planting season with something approaching optimism. According to the office's most recent field report, released Thursday, soil conditions across the county's eastern growing districts are running measurably ahead of where they were at this point in 2022, 2023 and 2024 — three springs that each brought some combination of late frost, drought stress or flooding that complicated the growing season before it truly began.

"The winter moisture was there when we needed it," said Gary Feltner, the county's agriculture extension agent, in an interview Thursday morning. "We had a couple of dry spells in January, but February came in wet and stayed that way, and the ground absorbed it well. Right now our topsoil moisture is in good shape and our subsoil is better than I've seen it in a while."

Feltner noted that soil temperatures in the lower elevation fields — primarily the flat ground along the Cumberland River corridor and the broader valleys of the southern end of the county — are currently running between 48 and 52 degrees Fahrenheit at a four-inch depth, still a few degrees below the 55-degree threshold typically considered optimal for corn germination, but on track to reach that benchmark by the final week of April if the current warming trend holds.

"The window we're looking at is April 22 to May 5 for most producers in the lower elevations. The ridgeline farms and the north-facing hollows will want to wait another week past that." — Gary Feltner, Harlan County Agriculture Extension Agent
2025 Planting Season Snapshot
  • Topsoil moisture: adequate to surplus across 70% of the county
  • Subsoil moisture: above average at all monitoring sites
  • Current 4-inch soil temp (lower elevations): 48–52°F
  • Projected optimal planting window: April 22 – May 5
  • Frost risk: remains possible through May 1 in hollow locations
  • Extension office spring workshop: April 17, 6:00 p.m., Ag Center
A Cautious Optimism

Feltner was careful to temper the positive outlook with a reminder that eastern Kentucky's spring weather can turn quickly, and that the frost risk window does not close entirely until at least the first of May in most parts of the county, and as late as mid-May in some of the higher hollows and north-facing drainages.

"I don't want people to hear 'good outlook' and go put their transplants in the ground next week," he said. "We still have frost potential. What we're saying is that once that window closes, conditions should be as good as we've had in years. That's a different thing."

The extension office will hold its annual spring planting workshop on Thursday, April 17, beginning at 6:00 p.m. at the Harlan County Agricultural Extension Center on Highway 421. Topics will include soil testing, cover crop termination timing, and an update on the state's grain storage assistance program. Attendance is free and open to all producers.

For producers who have not yet completed their soil testing, Feltner said the office can still process samples in time to inform planting decisions for this season. Tests can be dropped off at the extension office on weekdays between 8:00 a.m. and 4:30 p.m.
