Harlan County High FFA Chapter Takes Third at State Competition in Lexington, Best Finish in a Decade

Competing against 87 chapters from across the Commonwealth, the Harlan County team earned third place in Livestock Evaluation — the program's highest state finish since 2015.
By Meredith A. Holloway · Staff Reporter
Published April 4, 2025 · 8:15 a.m.
🏆
The Harlan County High School FFA Livestock Evaluation team placed third out of 87 competing chapters at the 2025 Kentucky State FFA Convention in Lexington. Photo courtesy of HCHS FFA Chapter

LEXINGTON — The Harlan County High School FFA chapter returned home Wednesday with a third-place trophy from the Kentucky State FFA Convention's Livestock Evaluation competition, the program's best finish at the state level in a decade and the culmination of what chapter advisor Donna Parsons called "the most prepared team I've coached in eighteen years."

The four-member team — seniors Kayla Napier and Dustin Howard, junior Brayden Saylor, and sophomore Abby Combs — competed against 87 chapters from across the state on Tuesday at the University of Kentucky's Coldstream Research Campus, evaluating beef cattle, swine and sheep for production traits and placing them in ranked order under time pressure with written justifications. Harlan County trailed first-place Warren Central by 14 points and second-place Calloway County by 7, scores that Parsons described as "splitting hairs at this level."

"These kids have been coming in before school since October. They drove an hour each way to look at cattle on other people's farms on Saturdays. Third place at state is not something you stumble into." — Donna Parsons, HCHS FFA Chapter Advisor
2025 State FFA — Livestock Evaluation Results
  • 1st Place: Warren Central FFA (Bowling Green)
  • 2nd Place: Calloway County FFA (Murray)
  • 3rd Place: Harlan County FFA ⭐
  • Total competing chapters: 87
  • Harlan County's last Top 5 finish: 2015 (4th place)
  • High individual scorer: Kayla Napier — 8th overall in state
A Long Road to Lexington

The path to the state podium began in earnest last fall when the chapter qualified at the regional level with its highest regional score since 2017. Parsons said she knew in the fall that the team had the potential for a special season, but that the real test would be consistency under pressure — something she spent the winter months specifically trying to build.

"Livestock evaluation is not complicated in concept," she explained. "You look at animals, you rank them, you explain your reasoning. What's hard is doing it accurately and articulately when there are 86 other teams standing next to you and a clock running. That's where we put our energy."

Senior Kayla Napier, who finished eighth overall among individual competitors — the highest individual finish by a Harlan County FFA member in recent memory — said the experience of competing at that level was unlike anything she had encountered in four years of the program.

"You've prepared so much that you're not really nervous," Napier said. "You're just focused. When it was over I looked at Coach Parsons and I could tell by her face it had gone well, but we didn't know how well until they called the placements."

Parsons, who has advised the chapter for eighteen years and has coached multiple district champions, said she considers this the most satisfying result of her tenure. "Not because of the trophy," she clarified. "Because of how they earned it. That part matters more to me."

The team will be recognized at the Harlan County Board of Education's next regular meeting, scheduled for April 14.
